What Do We Call This Thing?

Three finalists. Real rationale. Each name reshapes the whole system. Pick your one.

This app started as personal survival infrastructure — externalized memory, continuity scaffolding, a navigation deck for an overloaded brain. It's grown into something bigger: a modular cognitive support environment for anyone who's ever lost the thread of themselves and needed gentler infrastructure to find it again.

The name has to carry all of that. Not productivity-speak. Not hustle culture. Not optimization theater. Something that understands that people are messy, continuity is fragile, and systems should adapt to humans — not the reverse. These three made it through multiple rounds of naming work. They're all right. One wins.

The Three Finalists

WyrdWell
"The deep place you go to retrieve what you actually know."
🎨 Preview WyrdWell Themes

Etymology

Wyrd — Old English for fate; root of "weird." What happens because it was always going to. The thread spun before you arrived. Not doom — the inevitable shape of a life.

Well — The deep resource. You lower something into it and draw up what you need. Patient. Doesn't run dry from ordinary use.

Why It Works

Wyrd is what you were always going to be. The Well is what holds it when the fog gets bad. Together: you have a self that goes deeper than the current disorder. You don't defeat the wyrd. You see it, name it, and weave anyway.

The app is the well. Mnemosyne kept the well — the bards drank from it before writing existed. When executive dysfunction puts everything behind fog, the well is where you go to retrieve what's still yours.

ChaosCraft
"Not organizing chaos. Making something useful inside it."
🎨 Preview ChaosCraft Themes

Opens in new tab — see all colour themes for this brand

Etymology

Chaos — Not disorder as failure. Chaos as the actual operating condition. The thing that defeats order because it's better organized. The labyrinth, not the monster in it.

Craft — Deliberate, practiced capability you earn by showing up. Not talent. Not inspiration. What a witch does. What you build when you can't rely on the ground staying put.

Why It Works

Most productivity tools pretend chaos can be eliminated. ChaosCraft doesn't. The chaos is the condition. The craft is what you bring to it.

This is Rincewind's energy: not bravery, not a plan — just the specific skill of navigating impossible situations with your wits intact. Through the labyrinth. Not the exit — the thread back. The app doesn't promise order. It gives you the cord.

StoryKeep
"The fortress where your narrative is held safe while you're elsewhere."
🎨 Preview StoryKeep Themes

Opens in new tab — see all colour themes for this brand

Etymology

Story — Not fiction. Your actual narrative. The continuity of a life. The thread that makes it make sense. The thing that, if lost, makes everything feel untethered.

Keep — Both meanings at once: the castle's innermost tower, hardest to breach. And the act of holding. To keep a story is to tend it.

Why It Works

People don't just lose tasks or time — they lose the thread of who they are, what they've been doing, what it all adds up to. StoryKeep says: your story is worth keeping. The app holds it when you can't.

Your narrative is your compass. It points where you actually are. Rincewind always knows what story he's in — that's the only reason he survives it. The Chronicle, The Arc, The Refrain: this is the name about time as narrative, not time as task list.
